Fracture.

An information poster to visualize my rhythm game scores during quarantine.

A data visualization project to collect and visualize data from activities in our lives.

Fracture is a fascinating project about collecting data from our lives and visualizing them in poster form. As COVID swiped through the world, classes moved online, allowing me to return home to Taiwan. However, I was required to quarantine for 14 days. During those time, I collected all the scores from three different rhythm games I played, Cytus II, Arcaea, and Lanota. Then, I compiled the data and transformed them into a compelling narrative.

Timeline.

Spring 2020

Skill.

Data visualization, information design.

Software.

Illustrator, Google Sheets.

Contribution.

Individual project.

Create immersion by utilizing the entire poster margin.

I aimed to add immersion, so I full-bleed everything and used the entire poster as a scale. The different portions represent the time played for each game, and detailed information lies within each tile. In addition, I included a short description, key legend, and an image of the physical gameplay to provide context for the audience.

Poster details.

Grinding the game and gathering data by screen-shotting each game result.

Importing everything to Google Sheets to organize, sort, and brainstorm for narrative.

After collecting, I manually imported everything into Google Sheets. I recorded the game, score rank, accuracy (perfect, good, and missed), failed, full combo, high score, and the song title. It was a dreadful process, but it was worth the time since I could easily reorganize the table and create quick visualization to brainstorm.

Design a narrative, and transform them into compelling visuals.

Finding a style that best represents the three games.

Personal takeaways.

Successful data visualization must stay true to the data and communicate the “main idea” or narrative.

Assigning a narrative to data is crucial to create interest and adding value to the data. Without a good narrative, it’ll be hard for the audience to draw insights from your design.

Although this data collected is personal, getting help and feedback from others will always be helpful. They may have more innovative ideas as they are less attached to the project.
